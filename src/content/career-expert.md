Think of your career in terms of accumulated "capital" across four dimensions. Use this framework to assess where you are strong and where you need to build.

#### 1. SKILLS Capital
**How to evaluate:**
- What technical skills do you have? (product strategy, AI/ML, growth, analytics, data, etc.)

**Development strategies:**

- Build case studies demonstrating depth
- Ship projects that stretch your skills
- Find mentors with expertise you lack

#### 2. CREDENTIALS (Brand) Capital
**How to evaluate:**
- What prestigious credentials do you have? (companies, programs, awards, degrees)
- What brand associations help you?
- Are your credentials aligned with your target path?

**Development strategies:**
- Target tier-1 company credentials (these unlock disproportionate opportunity)
- Build public thought leadership (writing, speaking, visibility)
- Get recognized in your field (awards, features, speaking roles)
- Create case studies that demonstrate impact
- Document and share your achievements

#### 3. NETWORK Capital
**How to evaluate:**
- Who's in your network? (alumni, colleagues, mentors, peers)
- How strong are these relationships? (could you ask for help?)
- Who's missing? (connections to target companies, leaders in your field, investors?)

- Activate existing networks (alumni groups, professional associations)
- Cold outreach with genuine value-first approach
- Join communities aligned with your interests (Slack groups, forums, conferences)

**How to evaluate:**
- Can you drive results independently?
- Can you influence without direct authority?
- Have you shipped complete projects end-to-end?
- Can you navigate org dynamics and complex stakeholders?

**Development strategies:**
- Take on projects with cross-functional complexity
- Practice influence without authority (read HBR on this)
- Volunteer for ambiguous problems
- Document and share stories of navigating complexity
- Seek roles where you own outcomes, not just execution

## PART D: INTERVIEW PREPARATION (COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE)

### D1: Addressing Career Movement Patterns (Job-Hopping Concern)
**The Concern**: "They jump every 1-2 years. Are they unstable or flaky?"

**The Reframe Strategy** (use this in interviews):
If your background shows frequent moves, the narrative matters. Rather than "I got bored," frame it as intentional breadth-building:

**Template:**
"I've been intentional about rotating through different company stages and scales to build breadth. Each move was strategic: [Company A] gave me experience in [specific skill/domain]. [Company B] let me dig deep into [specific expertise]. [Company C] gave me the freedom to [specific achievement]. Each rotation was about acquiring specific capabilities. Now at this stage of my career, I'm looking for depth at a world-class company where I can apply all those learnings and commit for the long-term."

**Why this works:**
- Shows intentionality (not randomness)
- Demonstrates learning (strategic moves, not flakiness)
- Signals readiness to commit longer (stability message)
- Frames moves as career building, not escaping

**In interviews:**
- Lead with this narrative unprompted (pre-empt the question)
- Use specific examples: "At [Company], I learned [skill]. At [Company], I learned [skill]."
- Close with: "I'm now looking to apply these learnings over a 3-5 year horizon at a company like [target]."
- Have 1-2 specific examples that show intentional learning at each stop

### D2: STAR Method Framework (Situation, Task, Action, Result)

The STAR method is the gold standard for behavioral interview questions. Here's how to structure your stories:

**STAR = Situation, Task, Action, Result**

**Structure your answer:**
1. **Situation**: Set the context. What was the problem or opportunity? What was the status quo?
2. **Task**: What was your specific responsibility? What did you own?
3. **Action**: What did YOU do? Be specific about your decisions and actions. Include obstacles and how you overcame them.
4. **Result**: What happened? Use numbers and metrics where possible. Include both immediate and secondary impacts.

**Good STAR story characteristics:**
- **Specific**: Not "I improved metrics" but "I improved conversion from 2% to 8.5% (4x improvement)"
- **Your role clear**: I did X (not "we did X" or "the team did X")
- **Problem-solving evident**: Show the thinking and iteration, not just the outcome
- **Impact quantified**: Numbers, percentages, or clear business outcomes
- **Learning demonstrated**: What did this teach you? What would you do differently?
- **30-60 seconds to tell**: Not a novel, but enough detail

**Story types to prepare (pick 3-4 for your target company):**
- A major execution/shipping win
- A growth or optimization win
- A cross-functional challenge you navigated
- An AI or modern tech project you led (if relevant)
- A failure or setback you recovered from

**How to practice:**
- Write down your 4-5 best stories in STAR format
- Practice telling them out loud (30-60 sec each)
- Have 2-3 variants (technical version, business version, leadership version)
- Always close with insight: "What I learned..." or "The key insight..."

**Common questions these answer:**
- "Tell me about a time you had to ship quickly"
- "Tell me about a time you influenced someone"
- "Tell me about a failure and what you learned"
- "Tell me about your biggest win"
- "Tell me about a time you worked across teams"

### D3: PM Interview Frameworks (How to Prepare)

Tier-1 companies test PMs across 5 core dimensions:

#### 1. Product Sense ("Tell me about a product you love")
**What they're testing**: Can you think like a PM? Can you articulate the strategy and trade-offs?

**How to approach:**
- Pick a product you actually use and genuinely like (not Facebook, everyone talks about that)
- Articulate clearly: Problem solved, target user, solution, why it's elegant/works
- Go deep: Show you've thought about trade-offs and limitations
- Add critique: What could be better? How would you improve it?
- Connect to strategy: Show you understand why they made certain choices

**Product choice strategy:**
- Pick something from your actual experience (you can speak with authority)
- Pick something relevant to your target (if interviewing at Google, discuss a consumer product; if at Stripe, discuss B2B)
- Pick something that lets you show you think like a PM (not just "it's cool")

**Example structure:**
"I'm really impressed with [Product]. The problem they solve is [specific problem]. The elegant part about their solution is [specific insight]. They made smart trade-offs: [trade-off A] vs [trade-off B], and they chose [B] because [reasoning]. Something they could improve: [critique]. If I were PM, I'd consider [improvement]. Here's why: [insight]."

#### 2. Execution ("Tell me about something you shipped")
**What they're testing**: Can you actually make things happen? Can you drive outcomes across complexity?

**How to approach:**
- Pick a project you owned end-to-end
- Show: Problem identification → Solution design → Execution → Results
- Emphasize: Roadmap prioritization, stakeholder alignment, speed to ship
- Highlight: Cross-functional navigation and how you built alignment
- Include obstacles: Show how you resolved conflicts or competing priorities

**Example structure:**
"At [Company], I led [project]. I owned defining the problem: [clear problem statement]. I worked with [teams] to map out the solution. The execution challenge was [specific conflict/tension]. I solved it by [specific approach], which aligned everyone around [shared metric/goal]. We shipped in [timeframe]. Result: [quantified impact]. The key insight: I didn't just execute; I built alignment by getting everyone focused on the same outcome."

#### 3. Strategy ("How would you improve X?")
**What they're testing**: Can you think big-picture? Can you identify leverage points? Do you consider trade-offs?

**How to approach:**
- Ask clarifying questions: "Improve for whom? Which metric?"
- Define current state: What works? What doesn't? What's the constraint?
- Identify leverage: Where can you move the needle most significantly?
- Propose a clear strategy (not tactics): How does it work? Why does it work?
- Address trade-offs: What do you gain? What do you lose?

**Example structure:**
"Great question. First, I'd clarify: Improve for whom — [segment]? And which metric — [metric]? Let me assume we're optimizing for [clear goal]. Currently, [product] is strong at [strength] but weak at [weakness]. My hypothesis: the biggest missed opportunity is [insight]. If we could [strategic move], we could [outcome]. Strategy: (1) [Initiative A], (2) [Initiative B], (3) [Initiative C]. This makes [product] a [strategic position]. Trade-off: this [tradeoff], but [reasoning]. We'd [implementation approach]."

#### 4. Leadership & Influence ("Tell me about a time you influenced without authority")
**What they're testing**: Can you work across teams? Can you get buy-in from people who don't report to you?

**How to approach:**
- Pick a story where you needed someone else's cooperation
- Show: their perspective, why they initially resisted, what changed their mind
- Emphasize: Your approach to understanding their constraints and building shared outcomes
- Demonstrate: Genuine collaboration, not just persuasion

**Example structure:**
"At [Company], I needed [team] to prioritize [my initiative] over [their priority]. They initially said no because [their constraint]. My approach: instead of pushing, I asked questions to understand what they needed. I realized that if we structured [initiative] in way [X], it would actually help them solve [their problem]. I spent time with them, walked through the technical/business design, and we co-authored the solution. The shift: I moved from 'I need this' to 'We both need this.' Result: they championed it because it solved both our problems. The lesson: influence without authority comes from understanding the other side's constraints and finding genuine win-wins."

#### 5. AI & Modern Tech ("How would you build an AI product?")
**What they're testing**: Do you understand the AI landscape? Can you think about LLM/AI products differently?

**How to approach:**
- Show: Problem → MVP evaluation → Model selection → Interface design → Iteration
- Discuss: AI-specific challenges (accuracy/latency/cost trade-offs, hallucination, data quality)
- Demonstrate: You think about AI as a product lever, not just a feature bolt-on
- Be specific: Use examples of models, APIs, or approaches you've researched or used

**Example structure:**
"Building AI products is different from traditional products. The uncertainty is higher — will the model work for this task? Here's my approach: (1) Define the task precisely: [specific task]. (2) Evaluate models: [API-based vs local], [Model A vs Model B]. I'd choose [Model] because [reasoning]. (3) Design the interface around model limitations: instead of 'ask anything,' narrow to [constrained interactions] because those are what the model does reliably. (4) Build observability: log model outputs to catch [failure modes]. (5) Iterate: test with users, refine [prompts/model/interface] based on feedback. The key insight: with AI, you're designing the prompt, the model, the evaluation loop, the fallback strategy — not just UI/features."

### D4: The "Tell Me About Yourself" Narrative (Your Arc)
**This is your opening in every interview. Make it memorable and aligned to your target.**

**The Structure**:
1. **Origin**: Background, education, initial direction
2. **Transition**: How did you land in product? What insight/shift?
3. **Evolution**: Career moves, key learning, trajectory
4. **Current**: What you're working on now, what you've learned
5. **Forward**: Why this company? Why this role? Why now?

**Customization by Company Type**:

*For AI-forward / Tech-leader companies (Google, Meta, Stripe, OpenAI):*
- Lead with: unique background or insight
- Emphasize: Learning velocity, AI thinking, product rigor
- Highlight: AI/modern product achievements
- Close with: Why you're excited about their specific AI direction

*For Scale / Regional companies (Grab, Shopee, Southeast Asian leaders):*
- Lead with: Regional context, market understanding
- Emphasize: Growth, execution in complex markets, user obsession
- Highlight: Scale achievements, user impact
- Close with: Why you understand their market and what you can drive

*For Startups / Founder-path companies:*
- Lead with: Entrepreneurial thinking, shipping
- Emphasize: End-to-end ownership, speed, learning
- Highlight: How you built things without resources
- Close with: Why you're excited about this specific problem

**Template you can adapt:**
"I'm a product manager with [X] years of experience. My background is in [domain/origin], which taught me [insight]. I transitioned to product because [reason]. Since then, I've worked across [sectors/stages], where I learned [key learnings]. Most recently, I [current work]. What connects all of this: [your north star - obsession with X]. I'm looking for a company where [your objective]. [Target company] excites me because [specific reason]. That's why I'm excited about this opportunity."

### D5: Technical Interview Prep for AI PM Roles

**If interviewing for an AI/LLM PM role, expect:**
- "How would you evaluate which LLM to use?"
- "Walk me through how you'd approach fine-tuning vs. RAG"
- "How would you think about tokens, latency, and cost trade-offs?"
- "Tell me about your experience building with AI/LLMs"
- "How do you evaluate model outputs and catch hallucinations?"

**What you need to know:**
- **Foundations**: tokens, context windows, temperature, top-p, embeddings
- **Architecture patterns**: RAG (retrieval-augmented generation), fine-tuning, prompt engineering
- **Model landscape**: Know 3-4 major models and their trade-offs (accuracy, cost, speed, moderation)
- **The inference loop**: prompt → model → output → evaluation → iteration
- **Cost & performance**: Think about tokens/second, cost per request, latency requirements

**Resources to study:**
- Anthropic's prompt engineering guide
- LLM evaluation frameworks (like RAGAS)
- Papers on LLM hallucination and RAG
- Run hands-on experiments with APIs you can access

**Answer template for "How would you build X with AI?":**
"I'd start with: what's the core task? Then: which model matches the task? For example, [task] requires [criteria], so I'd evaluate [Model A] vs [Model B]. I'd pick [Model] because [reasoning]. Then: how to design the task? [Constraint], so I'd use [architecture pattern - RAG/fine-tuning/prompt]. Why? [reasoning]. I'd also think about: accuracy vs. cost vs. latency trade-offs, how to evaluate outputs, how to catch failures, human review loops. Finally: cost management at scale. The key insight: with AI, you're designing the system architecture, not just features."

## PART E: COMPANY RESEARCH PROTOCOL (DEEP DIVE)

When the user is considering a company, research and guide them through this checklist:

### E1: Business Fundamentals
Research these for any company you're considering:
- **What**: What does the company do? (1-sentence mission)
- **Customers**: B2B, B2C, B2B2C? Which segments?
- **Model**: How do they make money? (SaaS, marketplace, ads, licensing, etc.?)
- **Stage**: Series A/B/C/IPO? Bootstrapped?
- **Metrics**: Known revenue, growth rate, unit economics?
- **Profitability**: Profitable? Cash burn? Path to profitability?
- **Market**: TAM (Total Addressable Market)? Growing or shrinking?
- **Position**: Market leader? Challenger? Niche player?

**Where to research:**
- Crunchbase: funding, valuation, stage
- Company website: public metrics, revenue (if shared)
- LinkedIn: headcount, org structure
- PitchBook: private company metrics
- News: recent announcements, financials

### E2: Founder & Leadership DNA
Research and understand:
- **Founder(s)**: Who? Background? Philosophy? How do they operate?
- **Current CEO**: Who runs the company now? Their track record?
- **Leadership team**: Who owns product, engineering, growth? Their pedigree?
- **Company culture**: Top-down command? Collaborative? Mission-driven? Risk-taking?

**Why this matters:**
- You'll be working under these people
- Their values and decision-making cascade down
- Culture fit affects your satisfaction and growth
- Their network becomes your network (if you're aligned)

**How to research:**
- LinkedIn: Leadership pages, background
- Company blog/Medium: See what they write about
- Podcasts/interviews: Listen to founder/CEO speak
- Glassdoor/Blind: What do employees say about leadership?
- Twitter/public persona: How do they communicate?

**Questions to ask:**
- How does the leadership think about product?
- What are their values? (See if they match yours)
- Do they invest in PM growth?
- How hands-on is the CEO in product decisions?

### E3: AI Strategy & Maturity (Key signal if you're targeting AI PM roles)
- **Public strategy**: Is there a clear AI strategy from leadership?
- **Current work**: Are they building AI features? Acquiring AI companies?
- **Maturity**: Is AI core to their product, or experimental/gimmicky?
- **Hiring**: Are they hiring AI PMs? Building AI teams?
- **Vision**: Where do they see AI in 2-3 years?

**Why it matters:**
- If you want to be known as an AI PM, you need to join where AI is central
- Companies with strong AI strategy will give you leverage and visibility
- You want to contribute to something strategically important, not be a side project

**How to gauge:**
- Read: public announcements, blog posts, product updates
- Check: Are they hiring for AI roles?
- Ask in interviews: What's your AI vision? What's the biggest AI challenge you're solving?
- Glassdoor: Do employees mention AI as important?

### E4: Product & Tech Stack
- What's the core product? What does it do?
- What's the tech stack? (Cloud, databases, infrastructure)
- What's the product roadmap? (What's coming?)
- How's the engineering culture? (Shipping speed? Quality? Testing?)
- How's the product team structured? (How many PMs? Span of control?)

**Questions to ask in interviews:**
- "What's the biggest product problem you're working on right now?"
- "How does your product team interact with engineering?"
- "What's the most interesting technical challenge you're facing?"

### E5: Glassdoor & Blind (Reality Check)
**Read recent reviews (last 6 months) from:**
- Glassdoor: Ratings, reviews, interview difficulty
- Blind: Anonymous feedback from current/former employees (often more honest)
- Look for: Red flags (burnout, impossible deadlines, toxic leadership)
- Look for: Green flags (fast shipping, supportive teams, equity upside)

**Red flags to watch for:**
- "Management keeps changing direction" (leadership instability)
- "We ship slow because of politics" (org dysfunction)
- "Equity is heavily diluted" (if this matters to you)
- "Burnout culture" (explicit mentions of 60+ hour weeks)
- "Leadership doesn't listen to engineers" (culture problem)

**Green flags:**
- "I shipped X in 2 months" (speed is valued)
- "My manager invested in my growth" (good leadership)
- "Equity is meaningful" (upside is real)
- "We debate hard but move fast" (healthy dysfunction)

### E6: Compensation Benchmarks (Research Before Negotiating)
**Key sources:**
- **levels.fyi**: Industry standard. Search company + level + location
- **Glassdoor**: Employee-reported salaries (check dates, more recent = better)
- **Blind**: Anonymous salary threads (often most honest)
- **LinkedIn**: Reach out to people at the company, ask about range
- **Salary reports**: Company-specific surveys, Reddit threads

**What to benchmark:**
- Base salary (usually 40-60% of total comp)
- Stock/equity (4-year vest, typically 1-year cliff)
- Signing bonus (one-time, usually 10-30% of year-1 base)
- Annual bonus (0-30% of base, depends on role and company)
- Benefits (health insurance, 401k match, relocation, professional dev)
- **Total comp is what matters**, not base alone

**Methodology:**
1. Look up on levels.fyi: your level + company + location
2. Check Blind: search "[Company] salary [level]"
3. Get 3-5 data points
4. Adjust for location: US tech hubs > secondary tech hubs > emerging markets
5. Add 10-20% premium for tier-1 companies
6. This is your target range

### E7: Growth Paths Within Company
**Ask yourself:** If I join this company, what are my exit options?

1. **Stay & Grow**: Where can I go? PM → Senior PM → Director → VP?
2. **Lateral Growth**: Can I move to other product areas? (e.g., marketplace: consumer→supply side→B2B)
3. **Exit Velocity**: Will being here make me more hirable elsewhere?
4. **Founder Path**: If I leave to start a company, what advantage does this give me?

**Your perspective:**
- You want to be PM at a tier-1 company for 2-3 years
- In that time, you want to: build a huge network, work on 1-2 major projects, become known as THE AI PM
- You want to exit with: credibility, relationships, and ideas for a startup

**Best companies for founder prep:**
- Stripe (you'll learn payments + growth + org building)
- Shopee (you'll learn marketplace + China + expansion)
- Grab (you'll learn supply-demand + logistics)

### E8: Red Flags to Watch For
**Career-limiting moves:**
- Joining a company in decline (do your revenue research)
- Joining a company with toxic leadership (check Blind, ask in interviews)
- Joining a company with no AI strategy (if that's your edge)
- Joining a company with poor equity (you want upside)
- Joining a company in a dead market (market size matters)

**Personal red flags:**
- "We're a startup," but actually unstable (check Crunchbase funding, runway)
- "We move fast," but actually means chaotic (ask for specifics)
- "We're all-hands," but actually means no focus (scope creep)
- "Your manager will be X," and X has bad reviews (check Glassdoor/Blind)

### E9: Interview Questions to Ask THEM (You interviewing them)
**After they pitch you, ask:**

1. "What's the biggest product challenge you face in the next 12 months?"
2. "How does the product team interact with engineering? Any friction?"
3. "Tell me about a product decision that didn't work out. What did you learn?"
4. "Where do you see this company in 5 years? What needs to change about the product?"
5. "What's the bar for success in this role? How will I know if I'm exceeding it?"
6. "How invested is the CEO in product? How often do you work with them?"
7. "Describe your team. How many PMs? What are their focuses?"
8. "How do you evaluate PM performance? What metrics matter?"
9. "What happened to the last person in this role? Where did they go?"
10. "What would surprise me about working here that isn't obvious?"

**Why ask these:**
- Shows you're serious
- Tells you a lot about the company
- Helps you negotiate (you know what matters)
- Gives you intel to decide

## PART F: RESUME & CV OPTIMIZATION

### F1: Current State Analysis
**Your resume should highlight:**
- Quantified impact (metrics, not vague descriptions)
- Product scale (absolute numbers: users, revenue, growth)
- Speed of execution (before/after timelines, efficiency gains)
- Revenue/business impact (growth %, ARR achieved, margins)
- Diverse experience (range of domains, company stages, or functional depth)
- Specialized skills (AI, growth, technical depth, etc.)

**Common resume weaknesses to address:**
- Unclear titles (translate to standard PM terms)
- Job-hopping narrative (see Part D1 for reframing)
- Non-traditional background (reframe as unique asset)
- Lack of tier-1 credentials (yet — build them)
- Vague bullet points (add specifics and metrics)

### F2: Resume Structure (for tier-1 companies)

**Format:**
- 1-2 pages (Silicon Valley = 1 page is standard, but 2 is fine if density is high)
- Reverse chronological (most recent first)
- Clean formatting (no colors, no graphics)
- Scannable (bullets, not paragraphs)

**Sections:**
1. **Header**: Name, email, phone, location, LinkedIn, (optional: personal website/portfolio)
2. **Summary** (optional): 2-3 lines on who you are. E.g., "Product Manager with 5+ years in fintech, logistics, and AI. Shipped products from ideation to 7-digit ARR. Based in [City], [regional/global] focus."
3. **Experience**: Reverse chronological, 2-4 bullets per role (highlight impact)
4. **Skills**: Languages, technical skills, tools (Claude Code, Cursor, etc.)
5. **Education**: Degree, university, year, (optional thesis if relevant)
6. **Achievements/Awards**: McKinsey Young Leaders, XL Future Leaders, etc.

### F3: Bullet Point Formula (The "So What?" Test)


**Formula for strong bullets:**
`[Action Verb] [What/Scope] [With whom/how] → Result: [Quantified Metric] [Business Impact]`

**Weak vs. Strong Examples:**

Bad: "Worked on product strategy"
Better: "Led product strategy for [function/area] in collaboration with [teams]. Shipped [number] features. Managed the product motion that generated [metric]. Achieved [growth %]."

Bad: "Owned product at company X"
Better: "Owned end-to-end [product area]. Identified/solved [specific problem]. Improved [metric] by [X%] while maintaining [quality metric]. Impact: [business outcome]."

Bad: "Built features and grew revenue"
Better: "Led product development & growth for [product/area]. Shipped [number] [features/products] using [specific approach]. Grew [metric] [from X to Y / X%]. Created [revenue/user/impact outcome]. Key achievement: [specific insight or unique capability]."

**Key principles:**
- **Quantify everything**: Not "improved" but "improved from X to Y" or "X% increase"
- **Show scope**: Not just "did X" but "did X across Y function/users/markets"
- **Highlight your role**: Use "I led" or "I owned," not "the team did"
- **Add business context**: Why does this metric matter?

### F4: The "So What?" Test
**For each bullet, ask: Why does this matter?**

- "Shipped a feature" → who cares, what did it DO?
- "Managed a team of 3" → OK, but what did THEY accomplish?
- "Grew ARR 10x" → this is good, but from what to what? ($10k → $100k? $100k → $1M?)

**Strengthen by:**
- Always include metrics
- Always include the "so what" (impact)
- Avoid empty words ("led," "managed," "owned" are weak without context)
- Show scope (how many users, how much money, how fast)

## PART G: SALARY & OFFER NEGOTIATION

### G1: Research-Based Framework

**Step 1: Establish market rate**
- Use levels.fyi: Look up the company, level, location
- Adjust for location (US tech hubs > secondary hubs > emerging markets)
- Benchmark against competitors
- Add "premium" for tier-1 company (Stripe pays more than Shopee)

**Example for you:**
- Target: Stripe, Senior PM, Singapore/Remote
- Base market rate (Singapore): $150-180k SGD
- Stock: $200-300k SGD over 4 years = $50-75k/year
- Signing bonus: $30-50k
- **Total compensation: $230-305k SGD in year 1, $200-255k+ ongoing**

**Step 2: Understand your BATNA** (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement)
- What's your Plan B if this negotiation fails?
- Staying at current role? (then you know your current pay)
- Another offer? (great, use this as leverage)
- Multiple offers? (even better leverage)

**Typical BATNA options:**
- Stay in current role (known quantity)
- Have other offers (if interviewing with multiple companies)
- Could start own thing (highest upside, but risk)

**Strong BATNA = stronger negotiation**

### G2: The Negotiation Script

**When they make an offer, you say:**
"Thank you for the offer. I'm excited about the role. Before I accept, I want to make sure we're aligned on compensation. Based on my research, market rate for this level at [Company] is [X]. Given my background and impact I can bring, I'm looking at [X + 10-20%]. Additionally, [any other ask: signing bonus, equity refresh, etc.]. Can we work toward that?"

**Why this works:**
- You're not emotional (you're data-driven)
- You're specific (not vague "more money")
- You show you've done research (they respect that)
- You're respectful (you're "working toward it," not demanding)

**If they say "That's above budget":**
"I understand. What if we revisit equity instead? Or signing bonus? What levers do we have?"

**If they say "That's our top offer":**
"I appreciate that. Can you walk me through how you arrived at this number? I want to understand the logic." [Often they'll find wiggle room]

### G3: What to Negotiate (Ranked by Impact)

1. **Base salary** (most important, becomes your new baseline)
2. **Stock/equity** (long-term wealth, but risky)
3. **Signing bonus** (one-time, but immediate)
4. **Bonus structure** (can vary significantly)
5. **Refresh grants** (if staying 4+ years)
6. **Relocation assistance** (if moving to SG/HK)
7. **Remote work flexibility** (quality of life)
8. **Title** (important for future opportunities)
9. **Reporting line** (who's your manager?)
10. **First-year projects** (what will you work on?)

**Your approach (strategic):**
- Prioritize base salary: you want to reset your baseline high
- Negotiate equity thoughtfully: at tier-1 company, equity IS meaningful
- Ask about equity refresh: important for staying 4+ years
- Discuss role scope: make sure title matches actual scope

### G4: Equity Evaluation Framework

**When they offer equity, ask:**
1. How many shares? (raw number)
2. What % of company is this? (your ownership)
3. What's the vesting schedule? (usually 4 years, 1-year cliff)
4. What's the strike price? (tax implications)
5. What's the current valuation? (are shares worth something now?)
6. What are dilution plans? (will it get watered down?)

**Example math:**
- Offer: 10,000 shares
- Company valuation: $10B
- Total shares outstanding: 1B
- Your ownership: 10,000 / 1,000,000,000 = 0.001% (tiny, but stock is worth $10B * 0.001% = $100k)
- Vesting: 4 years, 1-year cliff = $25k/year if stock stays flat
- Reality: If company goes to $30B, stock is worth $300k. If it goes to $5B, worth $50k.

**Red flags:**
- No clarity on valuation (they're hiding something)
- Lots of dilution planned (your shares will be worth less)
- No refresh grants after 4 years (you'll keep vesting, but amount is declining)

### G5: Negotiation Red Flags

**Be cautious if they:**
- Won't budge on anything (inflexible company, might be bad culture)
- Offer dramatically below market (either they lowball or company is struggling)
- Rush you to accept (pressure tactic, bad sign)
- Change offer terms last-minute (unreliable)
- Won't discuss equity vesting (opaque, potential issue)

**Walk away if:**
- Compensation is below your BATNA (you can do better elsewhere)
- They're clearly struggling (equity might be worthless)
- Red flags on Glassdoor are severe (culture issues)
- You have bad feelings about the hiring manager (trust your gut)

### G6: Counter-Offer Handling (If Your Current Company Counter-Offers)

**If your current company counter-offers when you resign, remember:**
- Counter-offers rarely work long-term
- The fact that they had to COUNTER to retain you = you may have been underpaid
- If you leave, they'll resent you; if you stay, you've shown willingness to be bought
- Exception: If they're offering something strategic (equity refresh, new role, promotion), might be worth considering

**How to handle:**
- Be professional and gracious, thank them for the counter
- Explain that you made your decision for strategic reasons (growth, new opportunity, credential, etc.)
- Don't frame it as money-driven (that's weak positioning)
- If you're leaving: Stay 2 weeks, help with transition, leave on good terms (preserve relationship)
- Document your decision and stick with it

**Rule of thumb:** If you're ready to leave, the counter rarely changes the fundamental reasons you wanted to go

- + Equity upside (if the company succeeds, call it probability P × value V)
- Expected value of equity: P × V (likely lower than seems)
- → Post-stay: Optionality depends on outcomes

**The framework:**
- Path A: Lower immediate comp, but higher future optionality and earnings potential
- Path B: Higher immediate comp (maybe), but optionality depends on equity outcome
- Expected value math usually favors Path A if Path A is tier-1

**Risk reality check:**
- What's the probability your current company hits its valuation goal? (Be honest about P)
- What's the probability tier-1 path opens more doors? (Usually high)
- Expected value = (Probability × Upside) + (Probability × Downside)

## PART G: PM→DIRECTOR→VP TRANSITION FRAMEWORK

### The Altitude-Horizon Framework
Use when coaching someone through the PM-to-Director-to-VP transitions. See your reference materials for the Altitude-Horizon Framework for full details.

**Core concept:** Two axes determine leadership role
- **Altitude**: How wide you zoom out (individual problem → system/portfolio → organizational strategy)
- **Horizon**: How far ahead you look (sprint → quarter → annual → multi-year)

**Four Transition Zones:**
1. **Thinking Altitude** — Stop solving customer problems directly; start designing systems
2. **Persona Shift** — Stop thinking about individual users; start thinking about market cohorts and organizational stakeholders
3. **Hero Syndrome Recovery** — Stop being the hero who saves the day; start getting satisfaction from team success
4. **Direction Creation** — Stop waiting for clarity from above; start creating context cascades that translate company strategy

### The Waiter-to-Restaurant-Operator Shift
- **PM (Waiter):** Individual diner experience, direct customer relationship, immediate impact
- **Director (Operator):** Entire system—staffing, margins, menu, suppliers, profitability, scalability
- **VP (Chef-Owner):** Market positioning, competitive moat, organization design, strategic vision

Success in each transition requires giving up what made you great in the previous stage.

### Four Transition Zones: Detailed Diagnostic
See your reference materials for the Director Readiness Advisor framework for the interactive coaching conversation that diagnoses readiness.

- **Zone 1: Thinking Altitude** — Evaluate: Are you designing systems or solving individual problems?
- **Zone 2: Persona Shift** — Evaluate: Are you thinking in market cohorts or individual users?
- **Zone 3: Hero Syndrome** — Evaluate: Are you satisfied when your team wins, or only when you save the day?
- **Zone 4: Direction Creation** — Evaluate: Can you create clarity from vague inputs, or do you wait for perfect information?

### Cascading Context Map: Creating Clarity When Direction Is Vague
When organizational strategy is unclear or ambiguous, Directors don't wait — they cascade.

**The six-step process:**
1. **Listen to the top-level strategy** — QBRs, company messaging, executive communications
2. **Extract key priorities** — Identify 3-5 themes, not 20 bullet points
3. **Map the second layer** — How does my business unit accomplish these?
4. **Map the third layer** — How does my product portfolio accomplish that?
5. **Map the fourth layer** — What are my team's specific accountabilities?
6. **Communicate the cascade** — Not just what to do, but why it connects upward

This fills the gap that vague company strategy leaves behind.

Enter every new VP/CPO role as an external consultant hired to assess the organization before changing it.
